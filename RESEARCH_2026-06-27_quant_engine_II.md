# StockSage Quant-Engine Research II: Calibration Decision, Exits, Crypto Edges & Execution

## Executive Overview

This report consolidates four verified research strands into actionable, evidence-bounded guidance for StockSage. The unifying theme is that the engine's existing defensive machinery — its purged/embargoed out-of-sample (OOS) selector, NetEdge cost model, Deflated-Sharpe / False-Strategy-Theorem (FST) penalties, Wilson lower bound, half-Kelly allocator, and ATR trailing stop — is the correct foundation, and that almost every recommendation here is an *addition or gating refinement* threaded through that infrastructure rather than a rebuild. Concretely: (1) on calibration, add Beta calibration as a *candidate* map and let the existing OOS selector choose it per refit (it is mis-specified to keep a purely-isotonic calibrator on a sub-1000-trade journal, but a hard replace is not warranted); (2) on exits, keep the ATR trailing stop but reframe it as *regime-gated drawdown insurance*, not a free expectancy booster, because the academic record says stops are process-dependent and their value lives in left-tail truncation; (3) on edges, re-weight crypto momentum toward short-lookback time-series/ATR trend in liquid large-caps and apply conditional, liquidity-aware publication-decay haircuts to equities; (4) on execution, keep the flat-bps spread/slippage model as the binding retail cost and add a near-zero-at-retail square-root impact term that only bites for thin/large orders. Throughout, every headline academic return is gross-of-cost and must never be surfaced as achievable — the honesty floor (no guaranteed profit; all numbers labelled estimates with assumptions) holds.

## 1. Calibration: Beta as a Candidate Map for the Sub-1000-Trade Journal

**The core argument.** StockSage's journal sits in the sample-size regime where unconstrained isotonic/PAV overfits and a *constrained parametric* map wins; a purely-isotonic calibrator is therefore mis-specified for the current data volume. Beta calibration is the right parametric candidate because it is fit exactly as a bivariate logistic regression (no bespoke optimizer), it provably contains the identity map so it cannot "uncalibrate" already-good conviction the way Platt can, and head-to-head on 41 UCI datasets it was significantly better than isotonic on log-loss while statistically on-par on Brier.

**Key findings (with confidence and honesty corrections):**

- **[HIGH] Fit as a bivariate logistic regression — no new optimizer.** The 3-param map is `μ_beta(s; a,b,c) = 1/(1 + 1/(e^c · s^a / (1-s)^b))`. Set `s' = ln(s)`, `s'' = -ln(1-s)`, fit logistic regression predicting the binary outcome y from `(s', s'')` to recover `(a, b)` and intercept `c`. Clamp s to `(1e-6, 1-1e-6)` first — conviction of exactly 0 or 1 produces infinite features (Kull et al. 2017).
- **[HIGH] Identity-map safety.** Beta with `a=1, b=1, c=0` returns scores unchanged, so if raw conviction is already well-calibrated, Beta learns a near-identity and "does not make matters worse." Platt **cannot** represent identity and "can easily uncalibrate a perfectly calibrated classifier." *Caveat:* this is decisive only **against Platt** — isotonic can also approximate identity — so identity-safety alone does not justify switching from incumbent isotonic (Kull et al. 2017).
- **[HIGH] Head-to-head result.** On 41 UCI datasets (Naive Bayes + 2 AdaBoost variants, 10×5-fold CV), full 3-param Beta was *significantly* better than isotonic on **log-loss** (Friedman p = 6.9e-17 NB, 1.0e-12 Ada-O) and on-par on Brier; "no other method was ever significantly better than beta calibration." Authors call it "the preferred calibration method for smaller datasets." *Refinement:* on **Brier** the point estimate non-significantly tilts to isotonic — the gain is concentrated on log-loss (Kull et al. 2017).
- **[HIGH→MIXED] The crossover is an extrapolation for Beta.** Below ~200–1000 calibration points the constrained parametric method beats unconstrained isotonic (isotonic overfits); isotonic only ties/wins at ~1000+. But this crossover was measured for **Platt-vs-isotonic** (Niculescu-Mizil & Caruana ICML 2005, Fig 7, sizes 32–8192); **no Kull paper runs a size sweep**, so the Beta threshold is an inferred prior. The "a fortiori" framing is **backwards** — Beta is *richer* (less constrained) than Platt, so it *retains most* of Platt's small-sample edge rather than strengthening it.
- **[HIGH] Beta generalizes Platt-on-log-odds, not vanilla Platt.** Forcing `b=a` gives the 2-param map = logistic/Platt calibration on the log-odds feature `ln(s/(1-s))` (Proposition 1) — the known linear-in-log-odds (LLO) method. The third parameter (`a ≠ b`) corrects asymmetric/skewed score distributions Platt cannot.
- **[HIGH] Monotonicity is NOT automatic.** Constraints `a ≥ 0, b ≥ 0` (c free) make the map non-decreasing. An unconstrained fit can return `a<0` or `b<0` → a nonsensical conviction-up→win-prob-down map. Use a **non-negativity-constrained solver** OR the paper's drop-and-refit. Set the LR penalty effectively off (C ≈ 1e11) — default L2 shrinks a,b toward 0 and degrades the map (Kull et al. 2017; netcal reference impl).
- **[HIGH] Not in scikit-learn.** Beta requires the `betacal`/`netcal` package or a small custom fit on an existing LR routine. Beta gives **no native conservative lower bound**, so Wilson must be re-derived **on top of** the Beta point estimate (bootstrap the Beta fit's lower predicted quantile — Beta has no bins for a bin-wise Wilson interval) (Kull et al. 2017; scikit-learn calibration docs).

**Practices to adopt:** Register `{isotonic, full-3-param beta, identity}` as candidates and let the existing OOS Brier+log-loss-vs-baseline harness pick per refit (favor Beta/Platt < ~200–300 trades; allow isotonic only near ~1000+). Implement Beta via the existing LR routine with the clamp/feature recipe above. Enforce `a,b ≥ 0` and near-unregularized MLE to protect the high-conviction tail driving EV/ranking/velocity/weekly-R$/Kelly. Fit on raw 0/1 outcomes; keep Wilson as a separate floor (do **not** feed Wilson-shrunk targets into the Beta fit — double-shrinkage compresses the high-conviction tail). Optionally add an AICc/likelihood-ratio gate falling back to Beta[a=b] (LLO) for very small n — *labelled defense-in-depth, not a paper finding*. Thread the chosen map's win-prob through the single existing locus exactly as today. Validate against the netcal/Kull reference.

**Pitfalls:** Do **not** hard-replace isotonic on the crossover number alone (it is a Platt-vs-isotonic result — a category substitution to treat it as proof). Do **not** use the "a fortiori" argument (Beta retains *most*, not *more*, of Platt's edge). Do **not** expect a large gain if conviction is already roughly calibrated (this is also why the identity candidate matters). Do **not** trust a vanilla unconstrained, default-regularized LR fit (non-monotone or shrunk). Do **not** treat "best for small data" as a guarantee for this journal — the relevant test is parametric-vs-isotonic on *StockSage's own data* via the OOS harness. Do **not** claim "Beta strictly generalizes Platt" without the log-odds qualifier. Do **not** cite Alasalmi et al. 2020 as evidence *for* Beta (it motivates small-n safeguards generally; it proposes a data-generation method for isotonic and neither tests nor endorses Beta). Do **not** forget the Brier reversal — track both metrics and weight toward log-loss for the win-prob application.

*Citations:* Kull, Silva Filho & Flach, AISTATS 2017 (https://proceedings.mlr.press/v54/kull17a.html); extended journal version, Electronic Journal of Statistics 11(2) (https://projecteuclid.org/journals/electronic-journal-of-statistics/volume-11/issue-2/...); Niculescu-Mizil & Caruana, ICML 2005 (https://www.cs.cornell.edu/~alexn/papers/calibration.icml05.crc.rev3.pdf) and the boosting paper (https://www.cs.cornell.edu/~caruana/niculescu.scldbst.crc.rev4.pdf); scikit-learn calibration docs (https://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/calibration.html); netcal `BetaCalibration` (https://efs-opensource.github.io/calibration-framework/...); Alasalmi et al., ACM TKDD 2020 (https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3385656).

## 2. Exits & Trade Management: Stops Are Drawdown Insurance, Not Free Alpha

**The thesis.** Stops and trailing exits are **not** free risk reducers and do **not** reliably raise average R. Their expected-return value is process-dependent — negative under a random walk, positive only under momentum/positive autocorrelation or a low-return regime where parking in a safe asset wins. Where they add risk-adjusted value, the gain comes almost entirely from **truncating the LEFT TAIL** (cutting crash/drawdown months, lifting Sharpe) — not from improving the typical winner. The robust, cost-aware reading is "trailing + wide, gated by regime."

**Key findings:**

- **[HIGH] Stops are process-dependent, not universal.** Kaminski & Lo prove that under an i.i.d. random walk a 0/1 stop-loss *always* reduces expected return (the stopping premium is negative): `Δμ = p₀·(r_f − μ) ≤ 0` for any strategy with `μ > r_f`. Stops add expected return *only* under momentum/positive serial correlation or a regime where the safe asset beats a low-return regime. A trailing stop is a **momentum/regime bet**, not a free risk reducer.
- **[HIGH] The hurdle is autocorrelation ≥ Sharpe, not > 0.** Kaminski-Lo Proposition 2 requires per-period autocorrelation `ρ ≥ π/σ` (per-period Sharpe) for the stopping premium to turn positive — a high bar. **Gate the stop on ρ-vs-Sharpe, not merely on a positive trend reading.**
- **[HIGH] Where stops help, the gain is left-tail truncation.** Han, Zhou & Zhu (JFQA), US momentum 1926–2013: a daily 10% stop cut the worst monthly loss from **−49.79% → −11.36%** (EW) / **−64.97% → −23.28%** (VW), raised mean monthly return ~1.0% → 1.73%, cut monthly stdev ~6.0% → 4.67%, and **more than doubled the Sharpe** by removing momentum-crash months. *Caveat:* gains are **gross of costs** (break-even ~4.0–4.8%/month; a reviewer argues 300–600 bps frictions could erase the average-return benefit); the positive contribution is on the long leg.
- **[HIGH] "Stops clip winners" is backwards for momentum.** Stops convert a left-skewed momentum payoff into a right-skewed one. In crypto (Sadaqat & Butt 2023, 147 coins, 2015–2022) a stop-loss momentum strategy raised average monthly return to 9.13% (t=4.05), cut monthly vol to 21.36%, and flipped skewness to **+1.683**.
- **[MIXED/QUALIFIED] The crypto evidence does NOT validate ATR-trailing or a long-only sleeve.** Sadaqat & Butt use a **fixed ~30% within-month** stop ("trailing" and "ATR" never appear); the strategy is **long-short** and the gain is driven almost entirely by the **short/loser leg** (loser leg +14.2% → −1.4%; long leg barely moves 6.19% → 7.70%); results are gross of fees/slippage/short-borrow and strongest in small-caps. An independent **2025 follow-up finds equity-style risk-managed crypto momentum can be significantly negative.** Treat as qualified support that hard loss caps cut crypto crash tail risk — not proof for ATR-trailing or long-only.
- **[MIXED] Trailing beats fixed and wide beats tight — as a conditional, not a law.** Snorrason & Yusupov (Lund thesis, OMX Stockholm 30, 1998–2009, 5%–55% grid): 20% trailing best mean quarterly (1.71%); 15% trailing best cumulative (73.91%); best fixed (15%, 1.47%) clearly worse; trailing beat fixed at every level except the tightest 5%/10%; the 5% stop was the **only outright loser** (−0.12%). But this is one unpublished thesis, one market, one window, no costs, no significance testing, stopped-out cash left uninvested. Robust takeaway: **"wide trailing, tight is worst,"** risk-adjusted/conditional.
- **[HIGH] The regime-conditional stopping premium is real but mechanism-specific.** Kaminski & Lo report ~50–100 bps/month added during stop-out periods (US equity, 1950–2004) while cutting volatility — but via a **flight-to-quality rotation into long-term government bonds** (~−17% correlation), at monthly+ frequency. It is regime-concentrated and does **not** port to an intraday ATR stop on single names or to crypto (no safe leg, 24/7).
- **[HIGH] Chandelier default is ATR(22)·3.0 — a PRIOR, not an optimum.** LeBeau's Chandelier Exit (Long stop = 22-day high − 3·ATR(22)) is the standard reference; StockCharts recommends raising the multiplier for volatile names (worked HPQ example at 5.0). Qualitative directional guidance with no risk-adjusted-return data behind the specific multiplier. Note ATR(22) already scales the stop with volatility at 3.0×.
- **[HIGH] Sweeping the multiplier is a multiple-testing hazard.** Bailey & López de Prado: `E[max SR]` under the null grows with trials N via the Gumbel form `E[max SR] = √(V_cross-trial)·[(1−γ)·Φ⁻¹(1−1/N) + γ·Φ⁻¹(1−1/(N·e))]`, γ≈0.5772. Illustratively a backtest Sharpe ~2.0 from ~1,000 stop configs deflates to ~1.2. Carr & López de Prado is the on-point source for stop/profit-take sweeping. Prefer the exact Gumbel form over the crude `√(2 ln N)`.

**Practices to adopt:** GATE the existing ATR trailing stop by the regime-bias machinery (tighten in measured momentum, widen/disable in mean-reversion) on the `ρ ≥ Sharpe` hurdle — reusing the regime signal already in the Kelly allocator. AVOID running the ATR price stop redundantly on top of a signal that already exits on trend change (Clare et al. 2012: "a change of trend is the best stop loss"); make the ATR stop the crash-insurance backstop, not a parallel exit. Default to the Chandelier PRIOR ATR(22)·3.0, widening to 4–5× for high-vol/crypto names, labelled folklore-grade. Reframe user messaging in two channels: (a) expected-return effect is a regime bet that can be negative; (b) volatility/drawdown reduction is robust and mechanical but a risk-preference trade, not alpha. Report **realized left-tail truncation** (per-regime change in worst trade / worst rolling drawdown, stdev reduction, Sharpe change) as the headline benefit — NOT a promised higher average R. Estimate StockSage's **own** regime-conditional stopping premium from backtest+journal (do **not** port other-dataset magnitudes) and thread that one stop-value estimate everywhere, as the win-prob is. Charge round-trip + whipsaw re-entry cost through NetEdge before crediting any benefit. Route any multiplier sweep through the Deflated-Sharpe/FST machinery (count configs as trials, exact Gumbel form) — keep the Chandelier prior **fixed**, consistent with the no-parameter-sweep design. For crypto, deploy a hard loss **cap** as crash-tail insurance but flag the published edge is long-short and short-leg-driven (long-biased sleeve → much smaller, possibly negligible net benefit).

**Pitfalls:** FOLKLORE — "a stop protects your edge for free" (false under a random walk); "stops clip winners and lower returns" (backwards for momentum — the real cost is whipsaw/transaction cost on tight trails); "tight stops protect capital best" (the 5% tight stop was the only loser). OVERREACH — treating any specific multiplier (2× or 3×) as an established optimum; "the wide-band optimum is a law" (one thesis, one market); porting the 50–100 bps/month or crypto 9.13%/positive-skew numbers onto StockSage's ATR stop. MIS-ATTRIBUTION — assuming the crypto result validates ATR-trailing or a long-only sleeve. REDUNDANCY — running the ATR stop in parallel with a trend-change exit. PROCESS — optimizing the stop level on in-sample Sharpe without deflation (use exact Gumbel, not `√(2 ln N)`).

## 3. Durable Edges: Crypto vs. Equities — What Survives Costs Out-of-Sample

**The thesis.** Published edges in both markets are real but smaller and more fragile than their reputations once you net out costs and apply honest OOS inference. For crypto, the robust survivor is **time-series (absolute trend / ATR) momentum at short lookbacks (1–4 weeks), concentrated in liquid large-caps**; cross-sectional quintile-ranking momentum is real gross but eroded by weekly turnover and illiquid-alt frictions. For equities, the canonical TSMOM result is statistically weaker than advertised, and published cross-sectional anomalies decay ~26% OOS and ~58% post-publication, concentrated in liquid, cheap-to-arbitrage signals. StockSage's machinery (NetEdge, DSR/FST, OOS validation, half-Kelly with cost gate and correlation haircut) is exactly the right defense set.

**Key findings:**

- **Crypto cross-sectional momentum: real GROSS, fragile NET.** Liu, Tsyvinski & Wu (J. Finance 2022 / NBER w25882, Table 4): value-weighted top-minus-bottom weekly long-short returns of 2.7/3.3/4.1/2.5% at t=1.99/2.44/2.74/2.00 for 1/2/3/4-week momentum; 8/16/50/100-week insignificant (1,707 coins >$1M cap, 2014–2018). These are **gross, in-sample, weekly-rebalanced.** Independent OOS/post-cost work (Han/Kang/Ryu 2023; Starkiller OOS) finds it weak and largely eroded by turnover. *Confidence: high.*
- **Crypto time-series (absolute trend) momentum is relatively more robust.** Han, Kang & Ryu (2023): "Evidence of time-series momentum is strong, whereas evidence of cross-sectional momentum is weak"; once costs **and** daily intraweek liquidation are modeled, "many momentum portfolios are liquidated and many [strategies] with statistically significant returns earn insignificant profits." This is **relative** robustness — the liquidation warning applies to the trend book too. *Confidence: high.*
- **The cost caveat is from the source paper itself.** Liu/Tsyvinski/Wu (w25882 l.264): "this strategy does not take into account trading costs and the feasibility of short selling"; **no net-of-cost figure** is reported. Round-trip crypto **taker** cost ~25–30 bps (Binance base spot taker 0.1%/side → ~0.2% on fees + 0.05–0.15%/side L2 spread; note 0.075% is the BNB-*discounted maker* rate, not base). *Confidence: high.*
- **Crypto momentum alpha lives in large, liquid coins.** LTW (w25882 l.138–141): above-median-size momentum long-short earns a significant **4.2%/week gross** vs an insignificant **0.6%/week** below-median. *Caveat:* gate on a **live liquidity/spread/depth filter**, not a static market-cap median (above-median still included illiquid alts; thin alts show 100–600 bps spreads). Do not conflate with the standalone crypto **size** factor, which separately rewards small coins. *Confidence: high.*
- **Equities/futures TSMOM base rate is strong but GROSS and horizon-specific.** Moskowitz, Ooi & Pedersen (JFE 2012): diversified 12-month-lookback / 1-month-hold vol-scaled trend across 58 futures earns a **gross annualized Sharpe "greater than one" (~1.1)**, ~2.5× equity-market Sharpe; positive for all 58 contracts; effect "persists for about a year and then partially reverses," so lookbacks beyond ~12 months invert the signal. *Note:* the often-quoted "1.28 vs 0.38" is **not** a Sharpe comparison from this paper (those are a t-stat and a correlation cell). *Confidence: high.*
- **POPULAR-BUT-WEAK: futures TSMOM is weaker than its reputation.** Huang, Li, Wang & Zhou (JFE 2020): asset-by-asset regressions show "little evidence... in- and out-of-sample"; the large pooled t-stat "is not statistically reliable as it is less than the critical values of parametric and nonparametric bootstraps" (12.53/4.83 > 4.34). *Honesty note:* they do **not** claim TSM is unprofitable — it is profitable but "virtually the same as" a strategy based on the historical sample mean needing **no predictability**. The weak result is about **predictability, not profitability.** *Confidence: high.*
- **Equity cross-sectional predictors lose ~half their alpha after publication.** McLean & Pontiff (J. Finance 2016, 97 predictors): returns **26% lower OOS** (post-sample, pre-publication) and **58% lower post-publication**; the incremental ~32% is publication-informed arbitrage. The 26% is an *upper* bound on pure data-mining bias; Chen & Zimmermann put the pure mining component at only **~12.3% (SE 1.7pp)** — most realized decay is genuine arbitrage. *Confidence: high.*
- **Equity decay is NOT uniform — it concentrates in cheap-to-arbitrage signals.** McLean & Pontiff: post-publication decline is greater for portfolios in stocks that are large, liquid, high-dividend-yield, low-idiosyncratic-risk. *Refinements:* "highest in-sample returns decay most" is significant only in the published version (treat in-sample strength as a *softer* input); decay is reliably a **US** phenomenon (Jacobs & Müller, JFE 2020) — non-US factors largely persist. *Confidence: high.*

**Practices to adopt:** Re-weight crypto momentum to make **absolute-trend/ATR (time-series)** the primary input, weighted at least as heavily as cross-sectional rank; keep cross-sectional below the 0.65 trend-family cap as a secondary signal gated on positive after-cost EV. Use **short lookbacks (1–4 weeks) only** for crypto; suppress/down-weight 8-week-plus; for TradFi assets use ~12-month/1-month-hold and force the selector to invert/disable beyond ~12 months. Add a **live liquidity gate** (spread + depth + recent volume) to the crypto momentum path, feeding measured per-asset spread/slippage (12–36 bps liquid alts; 100–600 bps thin alts) straight into NetEdge. Never display raw academic returns (~3%/week) as achievable; keep routing everything through NetEdge; model crypto taker round-trip at ~25–30 bps. Strengthen momentum inference with a **bootstrap that preserves cross-sectional correlation** and require beating a **no-predictability historical-sample-mean benchmark**, not merely zero — and **reactivate the dormant full DSR** to apply DSR/FST to momentum strategies. Make the equity publication-decay haircut **conditional**: ~50–58% of in-sample Sharpe for crowded/liquid/large-cap/low-idio-risk US signals; haircut **less** for illiquid/high-idio-risk names (let the cost model do the gating there); use ~12% only when isolating pure data-mining bias. Scope the haircut to US equities (reduce/disable for international). Apply the full cost model **and** a liquidation-risk/intraweek-drawdown check to the **trend** book too — pair the ATR trailing stop with the Monte-Carlo ruin sim on trend positions.

**Pitfalls:** Do **not** give cross-sectional quintile-ranking crypto momentum heavy default weight (treating the 2.7–4.1%/week numbers as a live edge is the central trap). Do **not** surface any headline academic return as achievable. Do **not** treat TSMOM as a near-certain edge (pooled t-stat over-rejects) — but do **not** overcorrect into "TSM makes no money" (it is profitable, just not via predictability). Do **not** quote "Sharpe ~1.28 vs 0.38" for MOP. Do **not** use a static market-cap median as the liquidity filter, and do **not** conflate large-cap momentum concentration with the crypto **size** factor. Do **not** apply a flat publication-decay haircut or apply it to non-US factors (~12% is the pure-mining component, not total decay). Do **not** exempt the trend book from cost/liquidation checks (TSMOM robustness is *relative*). Do **not** use the BNB-discounted 0.075% maker fee as the cost baseline (taker is 0.1%/side).

## 4. Execution-Cost Modeling: Spread Dominates Retail; Square-Root Impact Only for Thin/Large Orders

**The thesis.** For a retail backtest, execution cost is dominated by spread + half-spread + slippage, **not** market impact: at retail order sizes (Q/ADV near zero), the square-root impact law crosses over to a near-zero **linear** regime, so the choice between exponents 0.5 vs 0.6 is practically immaterial. Keep the existing per-asset-class flat-bps model as the workhorse for liquid names, and **add** a size-dependent impact term that only bites when positions become a meaningful fraction of ADV.

**Key findings:**

- **[HIGH] The canonical temporary-impact model is the square-root law:** `I(Q) ≈ Y·σ·√(Q/ADV)` (σ = daily vol, Q = metaorder size, Y = dimensionless prefactor "of order unity"). The exponent is confirmed at **0.489±0.0015** across 2,299 Tokyo Stock Exchange stocks — "one of the most robust empirical regularities discovered in the last 30 years." In this regime impact depends on **total size Q**, approximately independent of execution schedule (Bucci/Mastromatteo/Bouchaud, arXiv:1905.04569).
- **[HIGH, with correction] The square-root form is the WRONG asymptotic for retail.** For very small orders (Q/ADV near zero), the same authors (Bucci, Benzaquen, Lillo, Bouchaud, PRL 2019) document a crossover to a **linear, T-dependent** regime where schedule-independence does *not* hold. Best practice is a **linear→square-root crossover** (linear at small Q/ADV, √ at larger), not √ everywhere.
- **[HIGH] Empirical prefactor Y ≈ 0.5–1** across mature stock/futures markets (Bucci et al. arXiv:1602.03043), point estimates near Y≈0.9. Honest defaults using **daily σ and full ADV**: Y≈0.5 → trading 25% of ADV at σ=2% gives ≈ 0.5×2%×√0.25 = **50 bps**; Y≈1.0 → **100 bps**. Bias-corrected estimates fall lower (a 2026 AAPL study: raw c=0.69 but bias-corrected c_eff≈0.34; EuroStoxx futures ≈0.5). Quote Y only with its convention.
- **[HIGH] The participation exponent δ is near-universal at 0.5.** The strongest evidence — the complete Tokyo Stock Exchange survey (Sato & Kanazawa 2024, arXiv:2411.13965) — finds ⟨δ⟩=**0.500±0.002** (σ≈0.07, mostly finite-sample noise) and concludes the exponent is essentially exact/universal. The broad cross-study spread (~[0.4,0.7]; LSE~0.62, BME~0.71) is largely **estimation error, not structural uncertainty**. A dissenting view (Zarinelli/Farmer/Lillo, arXiv:1412.2152) argues a logarithmic form fits ~5 orders of magnitude better.
- **[HIGH] Almgren, Thum, Hauptmann & Li (2005)** — the canonical broker-data calibration (~700,000 Citigroup US equity orders, 2001–2003) — **reject pure √ temporary impact at 95%** and fit a 3/5 (0.6) power law: `(1/σ)(J−I/2) = η·sgn(X)·(X/VT)^0.6`, η=0.142±0.006 (t=23). β≈0.6 is the transferable finding; η=0.142 is pre-Reg-NMS and must be recalibrated. **At retail sizes (v=X/VT << 1) both β=0.5 and β=0.6 yield negligible impact — the choice is immaterial.**
- **[HIGH] Almgren et al. model PERMANENT impact as ~linear in trade rate with a turnover-based liquidity factor:** `I = γ·σ·T·(X/VT)^α·(Θ/V)^δ`, α=0.891±0.10 (≈linear), liquidity exponent δ≈0.267±0.22 (≈1/4), γ=0.314±0.041 (t=7.7), where Θ/V = shares outstanding / ADV = inverse turnover. **Low-turnover/small-cap names carry materially higher permanent impact.** The δ=1/4 exponent is the least-precisely estimated (soft default); coefficients are US-large-cap-derived (orders ≤~10% ADV).
- **[HIGH] The √ FORM is universal across asset classes** (equities, futures, FX, options, Bitcoin) **but the PREFACTOR is market-specific.** For Bitcoin, Donier & Bonart (arXiv:1412.4503, >1M metaorders) find δ≈0.5 with a much smaller, differently-normalized raw coefficient Ỹ≈4.5×10⁻². Options show δ≈0.40–0.43. Practical scheme: fix the √(Q/ADV) form and δ≈0.5 everywhere; **vary only Y by liquidity tier.**
- **[HIGH, with correction] Almgren's model is validated only up to ~10% of daily volume.** Flat size-independent slippage is unsupported (impact is universally concave). *Directional correction:* impact is **more** concave (logarithmic, or √) at large size and crosses over **from linear (small) to √ (large)** — impact effectively **never grows faster than √**. So extrapolating √ to very large participation tends to **over-estimate** mean cost. Flag >10% ADV as out-of-model due to unreliability + tail/partial-fill risk, **not** a deterministic convex mean cost.
- **[HIGH] Backtested cross-sectional equity anomaly edge is systematically optimistic.** Chen & Velikov: the 2020 Fed FEDS working paper (120 anomalies) finds avg net ~**8 bps/month** (strongest 10–20 bps after data-mining adjustment); the 2023 JFQA version (204 anomalies) finds ~**4 bps/month** (strongest "at best 10 bps", combination methods ~20 bps). These **omit price impact** → upper bound; post-publication net return SE is only ~5 bps (net edge statistically near zero). Net spreads survive mainly below ~50% one-sided monthly turnover (Novy-Marx & Velikov 2016).

**Practices to adopt:** KEEP the existing flat-bps model (spread + half-spread + slippage + taker fee) as the **binding cost for the liquid retail base case** — do not replace it; at retail Q/ADV the impact term should be near-zero by construction. ADD a temporary-impact term with a **linear→square-root crossover** (linear-and-tiny near Q/ADV=0, √ when participation is non-negligible). FIX δ=0.5 everywhere and recalibrate **only the prefactor Y per liquidity tier** (liquid equities/futures Y≈0.5 conservative default to Y≈1.0 aggressive bound); present impact as a **labelled RANGE**, not a point estimate. PIN and label the convention whenever Y is quoted (daily σ, Q as fraction of full ADV); treat Y≈0.5 as the honest conservative default. ADD a separate **permanent-impact term increasing with inverse turnover (Θ/ADV)**, Almgren form, to penalize low-turnover Saudi/small-cap names — label γ≈0.314, δ≈0.25, α→1 as US-large-cap-derived estimates needing recalibration. Offer β≈0.6 as a dataset-specific equity alternative but do **not** hard-code η=0.142 (low-priority knob — immaterial at retail size). FLAG/penalize orders >10% ADV as out-of-model (justify on unreliability + tail/partial-fill risk, integrating with the existing heat cap and correlation haircut). WIRE the impact range through every component the calibration touches (EV, ranking, after-cost gate, velocity, weekly-R$) — one consistent cost number everywhere; treat low-per-unit-turnover gross backtest edge (below ~20–30 bps/month per unit of monthly turnover) as likely zero net. MODEL partial fills explicitly for the thin/illiquid tier (fill probability/fraction tied to participation; charge un-filled intent against missed-edge/extra spread-crossing). USE Chen & Velikov's ~4–8 bps/month gross-to-net erosion as the named justification for the aggressive after-cost gate and honesty floor (8 bps = 2020 WP/120 anomalies; 4 bps = 2023 JFQA/204 anomalies; caveat: US long-short equity numbers — the qualitative lesson transfers, the bps do not).

**Pitfalls:** Avoid a flat, size-independent slippage constant as the only cost (impact is concave). Avoid the pure √ form at retail sizes (use linear→√ crossover; √-everywhere over-states tiny-order cost). Avoid letting δ float per tier (overfits sparse backtests; it is universal at 0.500±0.002). Avoid treating [0.4,0.7] as genuine structural uncertainty (it is largely estimation error; the honest band is narrow and centered on 0.5). Avoid the arithmetic slip: with Y=1, σ=2%, Q/ADV=25%, impact = **100 bps**, not 50 bps (50 bps is Y=0.5). Avoid hard-coding Almgren's η=0.142 or γ=0.314/δ=0.25 as current/universal (pre-Reg-NMS US large-caps). Avoid claiming Almgren "validates" √ (they **reject** β=1/2 at 95% and use β=0.6). Avoid the backwards inference that √-extrapolation to large participation *under*-estimates cost (it tends to *over*-estimate; no evidence for a steeper-than-√ mean regime). Avoid transferring raw prefactors across studies (Bitcoin's Ỹ≈0.045 is differently normalized). Avoid mis-citation: do **not** attribute the wide [0.4,0.7] range to the Tokyo universality paper (it argues the opposite); the Bitcoin δ/Ỹ figures are Donier & Bonart (arXiv:1412.4503), not the Emilio Said theory paper; the 8 bps figure is the 2020 WP, not the 2023 JFQA (4 bps). Avoid assuming complete instantaneous fills in illiquid backtests.

*Citations:* Bucci/Mastromatteo/Bouchaud, arXiv:1905.04569; Bouchaud review (https://bouchaud.substack.com/p/the-square-root-law-of-market-impact); Bucci et al., arXiv:1602.03043; Sato & Kanazawa 2024, arXiv:2411.13965; Almgren, Thum, Hauptmann & Li 2005 (https://www.cis.upenn.edu/~mkearns/finread/costestim.pdf); Donier & Bonart, arXiv:1412.4503; Chen & Velikov 2020 FEDS (https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/feds/files/2020039pap.pdf) and 2023 JFQA (https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/.../945133D5A3ECEEAF466AEE91551FD225).
