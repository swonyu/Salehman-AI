# StockSage Quant-Engine Research: Validation, Sizing, Regime & Calibration

## Executive Overview

StockSage already implements a credible quant stack — conviction-to-win-probability calibration (Wilson lower bound + isotonic/PAV), half-Kelly sizing with a cost gate, regime bias, volatility targeting, correlation haircut and heat cap, an ATR trailing stop, a walk-forward backtester gated on pooled t>3, and a Monte-Carlo ruin sim. The research across four domains points to one consistent message: the engine's *architecture* is sound, but its *statistical honesty* needs hardening in two structurally weak spots and its *calibration choice* should change for the data regime it actually operates in. Specifically, (1) the single walk-forward point estimate and the fixed t>3 bar are both fragile and should be replaced by a Sharpe *distribution* (CPCV with purge/embargo) and a *trial-count-aware* Deflated Sharpe Ratio gate; (2) half-Kelly is well-justified by exact, named formulas but its real protection comes from sub-Kelly sizing plus the existing risk controls and the MC ruin sim — not from any "Kelly never blows up" myth — and edge/calibration error is ~20x more damaging than covariance error, so calibration is where the growth payoff is won; (3) regime-conditioning is theoretically justified and empirically delivers *risk reduction, not alpha*, and only if driven by filtered (not smoothed) probabilities with execution latency; and (4) for a discretionary score and a sub-1000-trade journal, **beta calibration** dominates the current isotonic stage on data-efficiency and identity-preservation grounds. Throughout, every closed-form result is an estimator with stated assumptions, not a guarantee — the honesty floor (no guaranteed profit; estimates labelled) holds.

## 1. Rigorous Backtest Validation: Walk-Forward vs CPCV, Purge/Embargo, and the Deflated/Probabilistic Sharpe Ratio

StockSage's validation currently rests on a single walk-forward point estimate judged against a fixed pooled-t>3 bar. The literature (primarily López de Prado, plus Bailey & López de Prado on DSR/PSR and Harvey-Liu-Zhu on the t-bar) supports two structural upgrades: replace the single historical path with **Combinatorial Purged Cross-Validation (CPCV)** to yield a Sharpe *distribution*, and make the significance bar a *function of trial count N* via the **Deflated Sharpe Ratio**.

**Key findings**

- **[HIGH] Single-path walk-forward is statistically fragile for three named reasons** (López de Prado; GARP Pitfall #9): (1) only ONE scenario (the historical path) is tested, easily overfit; (2) results are sequence-biased — reversing the sequence ("walk-backward") often flips the outcome, itself direct evidence of overfitting; (3) initial decisions use only a small portion of the sample (warm-up waste). StockSage's single point estimate inherits all three.
- **[HIGH] CPCV mechanics are exact and implementable:** partition T observations into N contiguous groups, choose k as the test set per split. Train/test splits = C(N,k); distinct backtest PATHS = φ[N,k] = (k/N)·C(N,k). Worked example: N=6, k=2 → C(6,2)=15 combinations and φ[6,2]=5 paths. Paths grow as N→T and k→N/2, so a long series can yield thousands of paths and hence a Sharpe *distribution*.
- **[HIGH] Purge and embargo are two distinct anti-leakage steps.** PURGE: drop from training any observation whose label window [t_j,0, t_j,1] overlaps test-set label times. EMBARGO: additionally drop the h bars immediately *following* each test block to kill serial-correlation leakage (widen the test label interval to [t_j,0, t_j,1 + h] before purging). Recommended embargo h ≈ 0.01·T (~1% of bars).
- **[HIGH] The False Strategy Theorem:** the expected MAXIMUM Sharpe of N truly worthless (zero-mean) strategies is strictly positive and grows with N: SR0 = √(V[SR_n]) · [(1−γ)·Z⁻¹(1−1/N) + γ·Z⁻¹(1−1/(N·e))], γ≈0.5772, Z⁻¹ = inverse standard-normal CDF. Numerically SR0/σ crosses the t≈3 region near **N≈1000 trials** — so a fixed t>3 bar provably breaks once enough configurations are tried. Use an *effective* independent trial count (real trials are correlated), not raw N.
- **[HIGH] The Deflated Sharpe Ratio is the PSR evaluated at SR0** (not zero): DSR = Φ( (SR_hat − SR0)·√(T−1) / √(1 − g3·SR_hat + ((g4−1)/4)·SR_hat²) ), with T = number of NON-annualized return observations, g3 = sample skewness, g4 = NON-excess kurtosis (g4=3 for normal). DSR rises with longer track record T and positive skew; falls with fatter tails and more trials. Common rule: require DSR>0.95. All inputs are already produced by StockSage's backtester.
- **[HIGH] The PSR denominator IS the standard error of the Sharpe estimator:** SE(SR_hat) = √((1 − g3·SR_hat + ((g4−1)/4)·SR_hat²)/(T−1)) (Mertens 2002 / Lo 2002 IID estimator, generalized by Opdyke 2007). So StockSage can compute SE(SR) directly and form a moment-corrected t-stat (SR_hat − SR*)/SE. NOTE: this assumes serially-UNCORRELATED returns; Lo's HAC adjustment is NOT in this expression, so autocorrelated per-period returns make SE understated and PSR optimistic.
- **[HIGH] The t>3 bar is a FLOOR, not a constant.** Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016) document ≥316 published factors and show t>2.0 (p≈0.05) "is no longer adequate"; their model recommends >~3.0, with BHY-FDR at 5% implying t≥3.18 (Bonferroni higher, ~3.78; cutoffs rise over time). CAVEAT: HLZ's t-ratios are per-factor cross-sectional tests with M=316 the academic factor universe; StockSage's pooled-t is strategy-level with its OWN trial count, so HLZ is a borrowed heuristic — the on-point tools are Harvey & Liu (2015) and DSR/PSR.
- **[HIGH] Naive data-mining manufactures false positives fast:** ~20 trial iterations suffice to discover a (false) strategy clearing the 5% bar (expected false positives = N·0.05 = 1 at N=20), even for walk-forward OOS tests. This is why N must be logged for EVERY variant and fed into SR0.

**Practices to adopt**

- Replace the single walk-forward point estimate with **CPCV as the primary backtest engine**: pick N and k so φ[N,k] yields hundreds-to-thousands of paths, then report the Sharpe *distribution* (median, dispersion, fraction of paths profitable). Keep walk-forward as a sanity cross-check, not the verdict. Mind the power tradeoff: larger N shrinks each group's sample.
- Implement **purge then embargo** explicitly: (a) PURGE training obs whose label window overlaps any test label time; (b) EMBARGO with h = ceil(0.01·T_bars), dropping h bars after *every* test block. Raise h if label-formation horizon or feature memory exceeds ~1% of the sample (h ≥ max label horizon).
- Add a **DSR gate alongside (and above) the existing pooled-t>3 bar**: compute SR0 from the False Strategy Theorem using cross-trial variance V[SR_n] and an *effective* independent trial count; require DSR>0.95. Wire SR0 to consume the pipeline's trial count N.
- **Log EVERY variant tried** (every parameter, feature, threshold combination), not just the winner. The full {SR_n} set is required to estimate V[SR_n] and count trials; without it, FWER/FDR/PBO and DSR are uncomputable.
- Estimate an **effective** number of independent trials (cluster trial-return series to approximate independence) rather than feeding the raw counter into SR0. Over-deflation is the safer error direction.
- Compute **SE(SR_hat) directly** on NON-annualized per-period returns and report the moment-corrected t-stat next to the raw t. Use NON-excess kurtosis (e.g. `scipy.stats.kurtosis(..., fisher=False)` or `pandas .kurt()+3`).
- Treat **pooled-t>3 as a rising floor** keyed to HLZ: start at t≥3.18 (BHY 5%), and let the DSR (which scales SR0 with N) drive escalation rather than hard-coding a number.
- Run a **walk-backward robustness check** (reverse the sequence and re-backtest); if results flip materially, flag overfitting and down-weight. Feed the CPCV path distribution into the existing Monte-Carlo ruin sim so tail estimates reflect path variance.

**Pitfalls**

- Don't trust a single walk-forward Sharpe as the verdict — sequence-biased, warm-up-wasting, "as easy to overfit a walk-forward as a walk-backward."
- Don't use a FIXED t>3 bar — once N is large the no-skill expected max Sharpe alone reaches t≈3 (around N≈1000). The bar must be a function of recorded N.
- Don't treat the "5% embargo / 50-of-1000-obs" tutorial figure as the recommendation — it's arbitrary; the recommended embargo is h ≈ 0.01·T, raised only for longer label/feature memory.
- Don't conflate CPCV's path count φ[N,k] with the DSR trial count N — paths measure ONE strategy's sampling variance; N is the number of independent configurations searched. Using path count for deflation is a category error.
- Don't plug an annualized Sharpe with a per-period T into PSR/DSR — SR_hat, T and the moments must all be at the same NON-annualized frequency.
- Don't use excess kurtosis in the PSR/DSR denominator — the (g4−1)/4 term assumes g4=3 for normal; the excess default (normal=0) overstates PSR.
- Don't assume PSR/DSR corrects for serial correlation — it's the IID non-normal SE; Lo's HAC adjustment is a separate term. Autocorrelated returns make reported PSR optimistic.
- Don't treat the False Strategy Theorem / DSR as exact — SR0 is an asymptotic extreme-value approximation assuming independent, roughly Gaussian trial Sharpes; a naive raw N misstates it. Label estimates as estimates.
- Don't treat HLZ's 3.18 as a refinement of their ~3.0 model number (different methods: BHY-FDR vs structural model; Bonferroni ~3.78), and don't assume M=316 calibrates a strategy-level pooled-t.
- Don't log only the winning variant — discarding losing trials destroys both N and V[SR_n]; this is the practice the ASA warns against.

## 2. Position Sizing: Full vs Fractional Kelly, Vol Targeting, Correlation-Aware Heat Caps, Drawdown Control

The Kelly literature gives StockSage a closed-form, named basis for its half-Kelly default: in the continuous-time/lognormal limit, half-Kelly captures exactly **75% of full-Kelly's growth at 50% of the volatility** and cuts the lifetime probability of ever halving capital from 1/2 to 1/8. The harder lesson: this is all asymptotic and idealized — Kelly gives no finite-horizon protection, and the measured Kelly fraction is dangerously unreliable because **edge-estimation error is ~20x more damaging than covariance error** (~100:3:1 for the log/Kelly investor).

**Key findings**

- **[HIGH] Half-Kelly is justified by an exact, named formula, not a heuristic.** Betting fraction c of full Kelly gives growth-rate ratio g(cf*)/g(f*) = c(2−c) and std-dev ratio = c, so c=0.5 delivers exactly 3/4 (75%) of max growth at 1/2 the volatility (Thorp 2006, Eq 7.6). QUALIFIER: c(2−c) is exact only for the EXCESS growth rate (r=0); with r>0 it's approximate (Thorp's S&P numbers m=.11,s=.15,r=.06 give ~88% of absolute growth at half-Kelly). Single-sourced to Thorp; secondary sources corroborate the conclusion, not the equation.
- **[HIGH] Overbetting is strictly growth-security dominated — never bet above full Kelly.** 2x Kelly (c=2) drives excess growth to zero (risk-free rate only); beyond 2x it goes negative. Overbetting lowers BOTH growth AND safety; underbetting trades growth for genuine security and is not dominated (MacLean-Ziemba-Blazenko 1992; MacLean-Thorp-Ziemba 2010). LTCM is the canonical blow-up. CAVEAT: the exact 2x crossover is a continuous-time/Gaussian approximation; with jumps/fat tails ruin can strike below 2x.
- **[HIGH] Drawdown probability is sharply nonlinear in the Kelly fraction — a closed-form knob.** For full Kelly, P(wealth ever falls to fraction x of start) = x (50% chance of ever halving); for fractional Kelly c it is x^(2/c−1). Half-Kelly → x³, cutting the lifetime chance of ever halving from 1/2 to 1/8 and raising "double-before-halve" from 2/3 to 8/9 (Thorp 2006, Eq 7.13). QUALIFIER: holds only under r=0, continuous-time GBM, no jumps, known parameters, infinite horizon — and is fall-from-INITIAL-capital, NOT peak-to-trough max drawdown.
- **[HIGH] For lognormal assets, fractional Kelly is EXACTLY negative-power utility.** Betting fraction α of full Kelly = maximizing −w^δ with α = 1/(1−δ) = 1/RRA; half-Kelly → δ=−1 (RRA=2), quarter-Kelly → δ=−3 (RRA=4). EXACT only for a continuous-time lognormal diffusion; Thorp (2008) warns the approximation "can be poor" even in the discrete lognormal case.
- **[HIGH] Kelly optimality is asymptotic — NO finite-horizon protection.** 700 independent bets each with 14% edge and ≥19% win probability turned $1,000 into $18 (>98% loss) under full Kelly; half-Kelly's worst case was still $145 (−85.5%). Half-Kelly has a 99% chance of not losing more than half vs 91.6% for full Kelly (MacLean-Thorp-Ziemba 2010; CAIA/Ziemba 2016). Fractional Kelly mitigates but does NOT eliminate tail ruin.
- **[HIGH] Kelly sizing is extremely sensitive to edge-estimation error.** Mean errors are ~20x as damaging as covariance errors and ~10x as variance errors (Chopra-Ziemba 1993, ~20:2:1). This EXPLODES for near-log investors: ~100:3:1 for the full-Kelly/log investor ("log investors must estimate means well to survive"). CAVEAT: in-sample certainty-equivalent results; Michaud-Esch-Michaud (2012) dispute OOS generalization to large multi-asset Markowitz — but for single-bet Kelly sizing, mean/edge error dominates.
- **[MIXED] Kelly/log has a genuine optimal MYOPIC policy** — validating single-period sizing, but conditionally. Myopia holds for LOG utility even under serial dependence (Hakansson 1971) and for ANY power utility under independence (Mossin 1968); fixed-fraction optimum independent of horizon under a stationary opportunity set. CORRECTIONS: under serial dependence the log rule needs the CONDITIONAL return distribution; "never risks ruin" (Hakansson-Miller 1975) is a measure-zero P(W=0)=0 statement, NOT protection against catastrophic loss. The theorems validate the per-trade FRACTION choice; they say nothing about correlation coupling, estimation error, costs, or discrete sizing.
- **[HIGH] Max Kelly growth depends on the strategy ONLY through Sharpe:** g(f*) = r + S²/2 with S=(m−r)/s; the Kelly investor behaves as a mean-variance optimizer (utility m − s²/2). This ties Kelly to StockSage's t-stat/Sharpe machinery: the entire growth payoff of correct sizing is captured by squared Sharpe — edge/Sharpe quality, not leverage, is what compounds. The c(2−c) curve is flat-topped at the optimum (slope 0 at c=1), so moderate underbetting costs little growth while sharply cutting variance/drawdown.

**Practices to adopt**

- **Keep the half-Kelly default and frame 1x Kelly as an OUTER CEILING, never the target.** Working size should sit well below it. Clark-Ziemba (1988) ran live trading at quarter-Kelly (25%).
- **Enforce a hard upper sizing cap at or below 1x of the ESTIMATED Kelly fraction.** Because overbetting is dominated and estimation error biases the measured fraction high, the cap should bind below 1x — bounding the overbetting failure mode that sank LTCM.
- **Expose x^(2/c−1) as a labelled drawdown-control knob** tied to chosen c, complementing the heat cap. Label it as an idealized continuous-time, r=0, no-jump, known-parameter, infinite-horizon, fall-from-initial-capital figure — NOT a peak-to-trough guarantee and NOT the same metric as the heat cap.
- **Prioritize win-probability/edge calibration over variance modeling** in the conviction→win-probability pipeline. Given the ~20:2:1 (~100:3:1 for log/Kelly) ratio, the Wilson + isotonic/PAV layer is where the growth payoff is won. Invest engineering effort there ahead of refining vol-targeting/covariance.
- **Shrink the Kelly fraction explicitly because edge estimates are noisy**, and tie shrinkage to estimation uncertainty — narrower (more sub-Kelly) when the Wilson interval / calibration confidence is wide. "When in doubt bet less" (Ziemba).
- **Anchor sizing to squared Sharpe** (g(f*) = r + S²/2): surface the Kelly fraction's implied Sharpe alongside the pooled t-stat so users see edge/Sharpe — not leverage — is what compounds.
- **Keep the Monte-Carlo ruin sim as the primary finite-horizon safety check** and report half-vs-full-Kelly survival statistics ($1,000→$18 full vs $1,000→$145 half; 91.6% vs 99% "no more than half lost"). The MC sim — not any no-ruin theorem — quantifies finite-horizon catastrophic-loss probability for StockSage's discrete, cost-bearing, estimation-error-laden setting.
- **Treat single-period sizing as theoretically sound for the FRACTION choice but document its conditions** (myopic optimality; conditional distribution under serial dependence). Real-world soundness rests on the cost gate, correlation haircut, heat cap, sub-Kelly sizing, and calibration quality — not an unconditional no-ruin theorem.
- **Document the half-Kelly = RRA-2 identity as exact only under continuous-time lognormal diffusion**, approximate (and possibly "poor", Thorp 2008) otherwise.
- **Attach honesty-floor labels to every closed-form Kelly result** surfaced to users — g(f*)=r+S²/2, x^(2/c−1), c(2−c) are idealizations worsened by jumps, discrete rebalancing, costs, and especially estimation error.

**Pitfalls**

- AVOID "always bet full Kelly to maximize growth" — NOT supported. Real edge estimates are uncertain, the growth curve is flat-topped near the optimum while the downside is steep, and overbetting an overestimated edge blows up.
- AVOID ever sizing above 1x Kelly — strictly growth-security dominated; the documented culprit behind LTCM-style disasters.
- AVOID treating 1x Kelly as the target — estimation error biases the measured fraction high, so "full Kelly on an estimate" can already be effective overbetting (if estimated drift is 2x true, full Kelly cuts true growth to zero).
- AVOID presenting x^(2/c−1) as a guaranteed or peak-to-trough bound — it's lifetime fall-from-initial-capital under idealized assumptions; conflating it with the heat-cap metric is a category error.
- AVOID claiming Kelly/log "never blows up" — 700 favorable bets → $1,000 to $18 (full), $145 (half). "Never risks ruin" is a narrow measure-zero / no-leverage statement.
- AVOID over-modeling variance/covariance at the expense of edge calibration (mean error ~20x covariance error). Counter-caveat: in LARGE multi-asset Markowitz problems Michaud-Esch-Michaud argue covariance error can dominate OOS — but not for single-bet Kelly.
- AVOID stating "75% growth at 50% vol" and g(f*)=r+S²/2 without the r=0 / continuous-time / excess-return scoping (for r>0, ~88% at half-Kelly).
- AVOID mis-scoping Thorp's "poor approximation" warning to non-lognormal assets only — it applies to the discrete lognormal case itself.
- AVOID over-attributing c(2−c) (single-sourced to Thorp) and the "sequence of bad scenarios" line (a paraphrase, attribute to MacLean/Thorp/Ziemba and Ziemba-Hausch generally).

## 3. Market Regime Detection: Improves Live Risk-Adjusted Returns, or Just Adds Overfitting?

Regime structure is real and has a named generative basis (Hamilton-style two-state Markov-switching) that explains fat tails, vol clustering, skew and time-varying correlation — so a regime layer is theoretically justified, not curve-fitting. But the documented, exploitable OOS edge is overwhelmingly **risk reduction, not alpha**: the best long-horizon study (Bulla et al. 2011) cut volatility ~41% while adding only **18.5 bp/yr** on the S&P 500. Two failure modes turn this edge into overfitting: using smoothed (future-peeking) probabilities, and ignoring real-time detection latency.

**Key findings**

- **GENERATIVE BASIS (high):** the canonical model is Hamilton's (1989) two-state Markov-switching — a latent first-order Markov chain (2x2 transition matrix) with a high-mean/low-variance "bull" state and a low/negative-mean/high-variance "bear" state. Cite this rather than ad-hoc thresholds. CAVEAT: it tracks NBER turning points well only IN-SAMPLE via SMOOTHED probabilities; real-time FILTERED probabilities are weaker — a citational/structural basis, not on its own evidence of a live edge.
- **PERSISTENCE IS CLOSED-FORM (high):** expected regime duration = 1/(1 − p_ii). E.g. p_ii=0.98 daily → ~50-day expected regime; ergodic weights solve P'π = π. CAVEATS: ~50 is the MEAN of a right-skewed geometric distribution (median ~34 days at p=0.98); sampling error is amplified (delta-method SE scales ~1/(1−p_ii)²) — report with a confidence interval.
- **THEORETICAL JUSTIFICATION IS SOUND BUT IN-SAMPLE** (high on stylized facts; mixed on the inference to live edge): Ang & Timmermann (2012) show regime-dependent moments reproduce fat tails, heteroskedasticity, skewness and time-varying correlation single-regime models cannot. But generative adequacy does NOT establish OOS value; Dacco & Satchell (1999) prove even the TRUE regime model can underperform a random walk OOS because small real-time misclassification destroys the edge.
- **BEAR = HIGH-VOL, ROBUSTLY (high):** the robust exploitable fact is volatility asymmetry — the bear regime has the highest volatility and the leverage/asymmetric bad-news response (Maheu-McCurdy; Ang & Bekaert; Ang & Chen). The high-vs-low MEAN separation is partly a mechanical artifact of fitting negative skewness (Kirby 2023); these models don't reliably predict returns. Key sizing off the volatility STATE, not an inferred return direction.
- **CORRELATIONS RISE IN STRESS (high):** cross-asset correlations rise in the bear/high-vol regime — diversification weakens exactly when needed (Ang & Chen 2002; Longin & Solnik 2001; Page & Panariello 2018; Ang & Timmermann 2012). A single static correlation estimate systematically UNDER-haircuts in stress.
- **MEAN-REVERSION IS REGIME-DEPENDENT** (high in-sample; mixed on live payoff): Ang & Bekaert (2002) document regime-dependent mean-reversion and that persistence drives vol clustering; multivariate RS models forecast modestly better OOS — but RS models "often perform worse than single-regime models" on some tests, and a parsimonious affine model "performs almost as well."
- **REAL-TIME LATENCY IS THE CORE LIVE HAZARD** (high methodology; numbers study-specific): filtered/online inference materially lags true changes — one HMM study (Nystrup) reports ~25-day median detection latency — and real-time switch counts can run ~2x the smoothed in-sample counts (arXiv 2410.14841, citing Bulla et al. 2011). Backtests using SMOOTHED probabilities peek at future data (Kim 1994 backward pass) and overstate live performance.
- **THE EDGE IS RISK REDUCTION, NOT ALPHA (high):** Bulla et al. (2011), a 40-year OOS Viterbi-HMM equity-timing strategy, cut volatility ~41% on average while adding only 18.5 bp/yr on the S&P 500 (up to 201.6 bp on the Nikkei, where it merely turned −4.30% into −2.28%), net of 10 bp one-way costs. The Sharpe gain lives almost entirely in the denominator.

**Practices to adopt**

- **Cite Hamilton-style two-state Markov-switching** (switching mean AND variance) as the named generative basis, backstopping any ad-hoc vol thresholds. Use 1/(1−p_ii) for an interpretable "expected regime length" and the ergodic distribution P'π=π as the unconditional prior.
- **Drive the regime layer SOLELY from filtered/online probabilities (data through t only), with a one-period (t+1) execution delay.** Never use smoothed (full-sample) probabilities in the walk-forward backtester — they inflate the pooled t-stat against the t>3 bar.
- **Make the classifier expect bear = high-vol and feed the EXISTING regime sizing bias as a down-sizing signal in the high-vol/bear state.** Key off the volatility STATE, not an inferred bull/bear return direction (the regime MEAN is the fragile part).
- **Regime-condition the EXISTING correlation haircut** as a one-directional floor (resize down, never up, when conditioning is uncertain) rather than swapping in a fully regime-timed correlation matrix — capturing the no-under-haircutting-in-stress benefit while staying robust to misclassification.
- **Budget the whole regime layer's benefit as a drawdown/volatility CUT** (a Sharpe-via-denominator improvement the existing vol-targeting and heat-cap can re-lever), NOT alpha. Re-estimate for StockSage's own assets — the 18.5 bp S&P figure barely clears a 10 bp cost gate, so apply the cost gate to every regime-driven trade.
- **Validate every regime-conditioned change through the walk-forward backtester and DSR/PSR + t>3 bar**, treating real-time misclassification (Dacco & Satchell) as the dominant modeled risk. Add regime-specific dynamics only when they clear that bar OOS.
- **Attach confidence intervals to any user-facing persistence/duration number** (delta-method SE ~1/(1−p_ii)²) and present "~50 days" as expected/mean, not typical — consistent with the honesty floor.

**Pitfalls**

- Don't treat in-sample stylized-fact reproduction or NBER fit as evidence of a live edge — even the true regime model can lose to a random walk OOS once you must FORECAST the regime (Dacco & Satchell).
- Don't backtest on smoothed regime probabilities — smoothing uses future data (Kim 1994 backward sweep); look-ahead-contaminated.
- Don't rely on the regime MEAN estimate as a return-forecasting edge — partly a mechanical artifact of fitting negative skewness (Kirby 2023). The robust signal is variance/correlation asymmetry.
- Don't model regime-conditioning as an alpha generator — the documented OOS payoff is predominantly vol/drawdown reduction (the 201.6 bp Nikkei figure is loss-avoidance on a still-negative stream).
- Don't compute "bear-market correlations" by naively slicing the high-vol sample — conditioning on extreme returns biases correlation upward even under stationary normality (Forbes & Rigobon 2002). Prefer a bias-aware/stress floor and condition on the bear regime, not just high vol.
- Don't apply bear-regime correlations/maximal haircuts UNCONDITIONALLY in calm regimes — that perpetually over-haircuts and turns hedges into directional bets. Use a state-switched haircut, asymmetric toward conservatism only when stress is flagged.
- Don't import the Bulla et al. bp figures (or ~25-day latency / ~2x-switch-count numbers) as universal constants — they're study-, asset-, frequency- and threshold-specific; re-estimate and re-run the cost gate.
- Don't add regime-specific parameters without paying the multiple-testing tax — extra parameters and the latency/false-positive tradeoff (lower threshold = faster but more spurious switches and turnover) are exactly the overfitting surface the DSR / t>3 bar polices.

## 4. Calibrating a Discretionary Signal Score into a Win-Probability: Platt vs Isotonic vs Beta

For StockSage's regime — a discretionary conviction score and a live journal that rarely holds 1000+ closed trades — the evidence favors a **parametric calibrator over the current isotonic/PAV stage**. **Beta calibration** (Kull, Silva Filho & Flach 2017) is a 3-parameter family that contains a log-odds logistic recalibrator at one extra parameter, uniquely contains the identity map (so it can't un-calibrate an already-honest score), and is data-efficient where isotonic overfits below the ~200–1000 sample crossover (Niculescu-Mizil & Caruana 2005; scikit-learn). For live drift, **Online Platt/Beta Scaling with calibeating** (Gupta & Ramdas 2023) gives a streaming recalibrator with an asymptotic adversarial-calibration guarantee and no per-deployment tuning.

**Key findings**

- **Beta calibration is a 3-parameter bivariate logistic regression on ln(s) and ln(1−s):** p_hat = 1/(1 + exp(−c − a·ln(s) + b·ln(1−s))) (Kull et al. 2017). Confidence HIGH. CORRECTION: the 2-parameter a=b restriction is NOT classic Platt scaling — it equals a logistic recalibration on the LOG-ODDS of the score, logit(s)=ln(s/(1−s)) (the "linear-in-log-odds" sub-family). Classic Platt is a sigmoid on the RAW score and is NOT a member of the beta family; they coincide only when Platt is fed log-odds inputs. So beta subsumes StockSage's logistic option at one extra parameter ONLY IF that option is already a log-odds sigmoid. Enforce a≥0, b≥0 for monotonicity.
- **Beta contains the IDENTITY map at a=b=1, c=0** — so a fitted beta map can leave an already-calibrated score unchanged. The Platt sigmoid family does NOT contain the identity and "can easily uncalibrate a perfectly calibrated classifier," yielding estimates "worse than the original scores" (Kull et al. 2017). Confidence HIGH (falsifiable). Caveat: with finite samples beta only learns *close-to*-identity.
- **Isotonic (PAV) reliably matches/beats Platt only above a ~200–1000 sample crossover; below it, isotonic overfits.** Niculescu-Mizil & Caruana 2005 (verbatim): "When the calibration set is small (less than about 200–1000 cases), Platt Scaling outperforms Isotonic Regression with all nine learning methods"; isotonic ties/wins only at "1000 or more points." scikit-learn restates this. Confidence HIGH. CORRECTION: ~1000 is a soft band (200–1000), not a hard cliff; below ~200 the case against isotonic is strongest, 300–1000 is genuinely ambiguous.
- **Isotonic outputs a stepwise non-decreasing function** — distinct conviction scores within one PAV block collapse to the SAME probability (poor resolution for continuous half-Kelly sizing), and it gives no informative mapping outside observed support. Confidence HIGH. PRECISION: "cannot extrapolate" is marginally too strong — scikit-learn returns NaN outside support by default, or a flat clamp with `out_of_bounds='clip'` (degenerate). A parametric beta/Platt curve is defined on all inputs — an argument for it on out-of-range convictions.
- **Calibration-method choice should follow the score's distortion shape**, diagnosed from the reliability diagram. Boosted decision-tree/stump scores and SVMs show sigmoidal distortion Platt is built to invert; Naive Bayes (and AdaBoost) push scores toward the extremes, where Platt can make calibration WORSE and beta is preferable. Confidence HIGH for the mechanism. CORRECTIONS: don't lump all "boosting-style" scores as sigmoidal (AdaBoost belongs with skewed-to-extremes); don't default neural nets to beta (classic nets were well-calibrated; modern overconfident nets → temperature scaling, a Platt variant, Guo et al. 2017). For a heuristic discretionary score, decide empirically.
- **Platt scaling has a built-in small-sample regularizer worth replicating** in any retained logistic component: fit against smoothed targets t+ = (N+ +1)/(N+ +2) and t− = 1/(N− +2) instead of hard 0/1 labels (Platt 1999; Lin, Lin & Weng 2007). This is add-one/Laplace smoothing under a Beta(1,1) prior; it stops fitted probabilities saturating at 0/1 on separable tiny sets. Confidence HIGH. Caveat: a one-pseudo-count fix, not a substitute for held-out/CV calibration.
- **Online Platt Scaling (OPS) updates the sigmoid via online logistic regression** and "smoothly adapts between i.i.d. and non-i.i.d. settings with distribution drift"; the calibeating wrapper makes it "guaranteed to be calibrated for adversarial outcome sequences" with no tuning, and "all OPS ideas" extend to beta (Online Beta Scaling) (Gupta & Ramdas, arXiv:2305.00070, ICML 2023). Confidence HIGH. Caveats: the adversarial guarantee belongs to the hedging variant (HOPS/HOBS) and is ASYMPTOTIC L1/ECE decay (~T^−1/4 to T^−1/3), not finite-sample; "no tuning" means one fixed hyperparameter set; the guarantee buys calibration robustness, not predictive edge.

**Practices to adopt**

- **Make beta calibration the default calibrator** for the conviction→win-probability map, replacing isotonic/PAV as the primary stage while the journal is sub-~1000 closed trades. It is parametric (data-efficient), contains the identity map, and handles non-sigmoidal/asymmetric shapes typical of heuristic discretionary scores. Fit as a bivariate logistic on ln(s) and ln(1−s) with a≥0, b≥0 enforced (continuous, monotone maps preserve bet-sizing resolution).
- **Implement sample-size-gated method selection:** beta (or a log-odds logistic with the Platt regularizer) for sub-~1000 trades; switch on (or blend in) isotonic/PAV only once the journal reliably exceeds ~1000. Treat ~1000 as a soft band (200–1000).
- **If any logistic/Platt component is retained, define it on the LOG-ODDS of the score** (so it's exactly the beta a=b sub-family and genuinely nested), and fit against the smoothed targets t+ = (N+ +1)/(N+ +2), t− = 1/(N− +2) to prevent 0/1 saturation. Do NOT apply these smoothed targets to the isotonic stage.
- **Keep and lean on the existing Wilson-lower-bound shrinkage** as the explicit small-sample safeguard — it already partially regularizes PAV overfitting and is consistent with wide error bars (a few-hundred-trade journal has serial dependence and regime drift, so effective sample size is below the raw count).
- **Add a diagnostic step before locking a calibrator:** plot the conviction-score reliability diagram on a walk-forward/OOS basis. S-shaped → log-odds Platt suffices; skewed-to-extremes/asymmetric → beta. Don't assume the score's shape a priori.
- **Select the calibrator by walk-forward OOS log-loss and Brier score** on the journal itself (purged/embargoed folds, consistent with the existing backtester) — data-driven and falsifiable, not dogmatic.
- **For live recalibration under regime drift, adopt OPS/OBS + calibeating** (Gupta & Ramdas 2023): run Online Beta Scaling so the map tracks the best parameters as outcomes stream in, with the hedging/calibeating variant for adversarial robustness. Scope it correctly: it keeps the win-probability honest under drift, it does NOT confer edge — keep honesty-floor labelling; the guarantee is asymptotic, so small-journal realized calibration error can still be large.

**Pitfalls**

- "Just use isotonic because it's nonparametric and most flexible" is NOT supported for StockSage's regime — its flexibility is a liability below ~1000 samples, its stepwise output collapses distinct convictions, and it gives only NaN/flat-clamp outside support. Defer isotonic until the journal is large.
- Don't treat "beta a=b == Platt" as literal — a=b equals a logistic recalibrator on the LOG-ODDS, not classic Platt on the raw score. Getting the input feature wrong breaks the nesting.
- Don't assume Platt is the safe small-sample winner just because it beats isotonic at low N — plain Platt is itself biased on skewed scores, can't represent the identity, and "can easily uncalibrate." The evidence-backed small-sample default is beta, not merely "switch to Platt."
- Don't group all "boosting-style" scores as sigmoidal — AdaBoost belongs with the skewed-to-extremes (Naive-Bayes-like) family where logistic/Platt can make calibration worse.
- Don't default neural-net-style scores to beta — classic nets were among the best-calibrated; modern overconfident deep nets → temperature scaling (a single-parameter Platt variant, Guo et al. 2017).
- Don't fit any logistic calibrator to hard 0/1 labels on a tiny set — on separable data the fit diverges and probabilities saturate at 0/1. Use the smoothed targets.
- Don't over-read the online guarantees — OPS/OBS+calibeating is ASYMPTOTIC adversarial calibration via the randomized hedging variant, not finite-sample; the best-performing tracking variant (TOPS/TOBS) has no proven bound; "no tuning" means one fixed set. Adversarial-robust calibration is not predictive edge.
- Don't state "isotonic cannot extrapolate" as absolute — it can clamp (degenerately); the substantive conclusion (no informative out-of-support mapping, unlike beta/Platt) stands.

## 5. Build Implications for StockSage

Prioritized, deduplicated checklist. Each item is tagged `[validation|sizing|regime|calibration]`, ordered by expected impact-to-effort (highest first), and marked **NEW** or **ALREADY** (per the engine context). The honesty floor governs all of it: these are estimators with stated assumptions, not guarantees — no guaranteed-profit claims, all numbers labelled as estimates with wide error bars.

**Tier 1 — High impact, low-to-moderate effort (do first)**

1. `[validation]` **NEW — Log every variant tried** (parameters, features, thresholds), not just the winner. This is the cheap prerequisite that *unblocks* the DSR, FDR/FWER, and PBO; without it they are uncomputable. Highest impact-to-effort because it costs little and gates everything in Tier 2.
2. `[calibration]` **NEW — Replace isotonic/PAV with beta calibration as the default** conviction→win-probability stage while the journal is sub-~1000 trades. Parametric, data-efficient, contains the identity map (won't un-calibrate an honest score), continuous (preserves half-Kelly bet-sizing resolution). Fit on ln(s), ln(1−s) with a≥0, b≥0. Combined with finding #5 below, this is the single most impactful change because edge/calibration error is ~20x more damaging than covariance error.
3. `[calibration]` **ALREADY (keep & lean on) — Wilson lower-bound shrinkage** as the explicit small-sample safeguard; it already partially regularizes the PAV overfitting the literature measures. Keep it and use it to drive #5.
4. `[validation]` **NEW — Compute SE(SR) directly and report a moment-corrected t-stat** next to the raw t, using NON-annualized returns and NON-excess kurtosis (g4=3 for normal). Small code change; immediately exposes when skew/kurtosis inflate the standard error.
5. `[sizing]` **ALREADY (extend) — Make the Kelly fraction adaptive to calibration uncertainty:** shrink further (more sub-Kelly) when the Wilson interval / calibration confidence is wide. The half-Kelly default already shrinks; this ties shrinkage to estimation noise, directly addressing the ~20:2:1 (~100:3:1 for log/Kelly) mean-error sensitivity.
6. `[validation]` **NEW — Run a walk-backward robustness check** (reverse the sequence, re-backtest). Cheap; a material flip is direct evidence of overfitting and should down-weight the strategy.

**Tier 2 — High impact, higher effort**

7. `[validation]` **NEW — Add a Deflated Sharpe Ratio gate above the pooled-t bar.** Compute SR0 from the False Strategy Theorem using cross-trial variance V[SR_n] and an *effective* independent trial count; require DSR>0.95. This is the principled fix for the fact that a fixed t>3 bar provably breaks near N≈1000 trials. Depends on #1.
8. `[validation]` **NEW — Replace the single walk-forward point estimate with CPCV** as the primary backtest engine (report a Sharpe distribution: median, dispersion, fraction profitable). Keep walk-forward as a sanity cross-check. Implement purge then embargo (h=ceil(0.01·T), raised to ≥ max label horizon) around every test block.
9. `[validation]` **ALREADY (upgrade) — Treat the pooled-t>3 bar as a rising floor.** Start at t≥3.18 (HLZ BHY-FDR at 5%) and let the DSR (#7) drive escalation as trials accumulate. Don't hard-code a constant.
10. `[regime]` **ALREADY (re-wire) — Drive the regime layer solely from filtered/online probabilities with a t+1 execution delay**, and key the existing regime sizing bias off the volatility STATE (bear=high-vol → down-size), not an inferred return direction. The mean estimate is the fragile, partly-artifactual part; never backtest on smoothed probabilities.
11. `[regime]` **ALREADY (re-wire) — Regime-condition the existing correlation haircut** as a one-directional stress floor (resize down, never up, when uncertain). Captures the no-under-haircutting-in-stress benefit while staying robust to misclassification. Avoid naive high-vol slicing (upward-biased per Forbes & Rigobon) and unconditional bear-correlations in calm regimes.

**Tier 3 — Moderate impact / supporting**

12. `[sizing]` **ALREADY (keep, enrich) — Monte-Carlo ruin sim as the primary finite-horizon safety check;** feed it the CPCV path distribution (#8) and report half-vs-full-Kelly survival stats. This — not any no-ruin theorem — is the right tool for finite-horizon catastrophic-loss probability.
13. `[sizing]` **ALREADY (formalize) — Enforce a hard sizing cap at or below 1x of the *estimated* Kelly fraction**, never above. Overbetting is growth-security dominated and estimation error already biases the measured fraction high; this bounds the LTCM failure mode. Frame 1x as an outer ceiling, half-Kelly (or less) as the working target.
14. `[regime]` **NEW — Budget the regime layer's benefit as a drawdown/volatility cut, not alpha**, and re-run the existing cost gate on every regime-driven trade (the 18.5 bp S&P estimate barely clears a 10 bp cost gate). Re-estimate magnitudes for StockSage's own universe — don't import study-specific constants.
15. `[calibration]` **NEW — Add OPS/OBS + calibeating (Online Beta Scaling) for live recalibration under regime drift.** Keeps the win-probability honest as outcomes stream in; scope explicitly as calibration robustness, NOT edge (the guarantee is asymptotic).
16. `[calibration]` **NEW — Sample-size-gated calibrator selection:** beta (or log-odds Platt with smoothed targets t+=(N+ +1)/(N+ +2), t−=1/(N− +2)) below ~1000 trades; blend/switch to isotonic only above ~1000. Choose via walk-forward OOS log-loss and Brier on purged/embargoed folds.
17. `[sizing]` **NEW — Surface the implied-Sharpe framing** (g(f*)=r+S²/2) next to the pooled t-stat, so users see edge/Sharpe quality — not leverage — is what compounds. Optionally expose x^(2/c−1) as a *labelled* lifetime fall-from-initial-capital knob (explicitly NOT a peak-to-trough guarantee, NOT the heat-cap metric).

**Honesty-floor reminders (apply across all tiers):** Label every closed-form result as an idealization — c(2−c)/"75% growth at 50% vol" and x^(2/c−1) are continuous-time, r=0, no-jump, known-parameter approximations worsened by jumps, costs, discrete rebalancing and (especially) estimation error; SR0/DSR is an asymptotic extreme-value approximation; PSR/DSR does NOT correct for serial correlation (autocorrelated returns make it optimistic); regime persistence "~50 days" is a right-skewed mean with wide CIs; online calibration guarantees are asymptotic, not finite-sample; and HLZ's M=316 factor universe does not directly calibrate a strategy-level pooled-t. No item on this list promises profit — they reduce overfitting, bound downside, and keep probabilities honest.
